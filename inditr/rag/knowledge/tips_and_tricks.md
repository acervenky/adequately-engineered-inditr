# Tax Planning Tips & Tricks — AY 2026-27 (CA-Level Guidance)

## Section 89(1) — Relief for Salary Arrears

If you received salary arrears, gratuity, or leave encashment in FY 2025-26 for services rendered in earlier years, you may be taxed at a higher slab due to the lump-sum receipt. Section 89(1) provides relief to offset this.

**How it works**: The IT department allows you to spread the arrears over the years they were actually earned and compute tax as if they were received in those years. The difference is the relief under 89(1).

**Critical step**: File **Form 10E** on the IT portal (under e-File → Income Tax Forms) **BEFORE** filing your ITR. Failure to file Form 10E before ITR will result in disallowance of the relief and a demand notice.

Eligible income: salary arrears, leave salary, gratuity, commuted pension, compensation for termination.

## EPF Taxable Interest — The ₹2.5L Annual Trap

Employee Provident Fund (EPF) contributions and interest are EEE (Exempt-Exempt-Exempt) — **but only up to ₹2.5L of the EMPLOYEE'S OWN CONTRIBUTION per year**.

From FY 2021-22 onwards (Finance Act 2021):
- If YOUR (employee) contribution to EPF exceeds ₹2.5L/year, the interest on the EXCESS is taxable at your slab rate.
- Employer's contribution has a separate limit (₹7.5L per year across EPF + NPS + superannuation).
- For most salaried employees (12% of basic ≤ ₹2.5L/year), this is NOT an issue.
- Affects primarily high-salary employees (Basic > ~₹1.74L/month) or those making extra VPF contributions.

**Action**: If you contribute to VPF, check if total own contribution exceeds ₹2.5L. If yes, your Form 16 should show the interest as taxable. Report it under "Income from Other Sources".

## Sovereign Gold Bond (SGB) — Best Tax-Efficient Gold Investment

SGBs are government-issued bonds denominated in gold (1 unit = 1 gram gold price).

**Tax treatment**:
- **Held to maturity (8 years): ZERO TAX** — capital appreciation is completely exempt from capital gains tax, provided you subscribed at the original issue price (see Budget 2026 condition below).
- **Sold on stock exchange before maturity**: Treated as LTCG (if held > 12 months) at 12.5% or STCG at 20%.
- **Annual interest of 2.5%**: Taxable at your slab rate every year.

**Budget 2026 change (effective April 1, 2026 → AY 2027-28 onwards)**:
- 8-year tax-free redemption is now **only available if you subscribed at the original government issue price**.
- SGBs purchased on the secondary market (stock exchange) will be taxed as normal LTCG (12.5%) at maturity — the 8-year exemption no longer applies to secondary market buyers.
- For **FY 2025-26 SGB redemptions** (filing AY 2026-27 now): the old rule still applies — all 8-year holders including secondary market purchasers are exempt.

**Why it beats physical gold or gold ETF (when subscribing at original issue)**:
- Physical gold: 12.5% LTCG on sale after 24 months.
- Gold ETF: Same LTCG rates as physical gold.
- SGB at 8 years (original subscriber): 0% on appreciation. For long-term gold allocation, SGB at original issue is unambiguously the best tax structure.

## Section 80E — Education Loan Interest (Massively Underused)

Section 80E allows deduction of the FULL interest paid on education loans with NO monetary cap.

**Eligible loans**: Any loan taken from a bank or approved financial institution for higher education (graduation, postgraduate, professional courses) for self, spouse, children, or legal wards.

**Duration**: 8 consecutive assessment years beginning from the year repayment starts, or until full repayment, whichever is earlier.

**Old regime only**. But with no cap and often high interest amounts, this is often the most valuable unused deduction for young professionals repaying study loans.

Example: ₹5L interest paid on an education loan → ₹5L deduction → saves ₹1.5L at 30% slab.

## Section 80G — Donations

Donations to approved institutions are deductible under Section 80G.

**100% deduction (no limit)**:
- PM National Relief Fund, PM CARES Fund
- Chief Minister's Relief Fund, Lt. Governor's Relief Fund
- National Foundation for Communal Harmony
- Approved universities/educational institutions of national eminence

**50% deduction (subject to 10% of Adjusted GTI cap)**:
- Most charitable trusts, NGOs with 80G registration
- "Adjusted GTI" = Gross Total Income minus long-term CG, special income, and deductions (except 80G itself)

**Requirements**: Get Form 80G receipt from the institution. Verify their 80G registration is valid (search on IT portal). Cash donations above ₹2,000 are NOT eligible.

## Form 12BB — Employer TDS Optimisation

If you're on the old regime, submit **Form 12BB** to your employer at the start of each financial year.

Form 12BB declares:
- HRA details (rent amount, landlord name & PAN)
- Home loan interest (bank certificate)
- 80C investments (LIC receipts, ELSS statements, PPF passbook)
- 80D health insurance premium receipts
- Any other deductions

Without Form 12BB, your employer will deduct higher TDS (they'll assume no deductions). You can still claim deductions at filing time, but you'd need to pay the difference and possibly interest on underpayment.

## LTCG Harvesting Strategy — Do It Every Year

The ₹1,25,000 annual exemption on equity/equity-MF LTCG (Section 112A) **does not carry forward**. Use it or lose it each year.

**Strategy**:
1. In March each year, check your portfolio for any equity/MF gains held > 12 months.
2. Book up to ₹1,25,000 in LTCG by selling.
3. Immediately rebuy the same units (no wash-sale rule in India — perfectly legal).
4. Your new cost basis resets to the higher price, reducing future taxable gains.

Over 10 years, this strategy can save ₹1-2L+ in total tax for a typical investor with a growing equity portfolio.

## NPS — Dual Deduction Benefit

NPS Tier-1 offers a unique dual deduction that many miss:
- **80CCD(1)**: Your own NPS contribution up to 10% of salary, within the ₹1.5L 80C limit.
- **80CCD(1B)**: ADDITIONAL ₹50,000 deduction, completely separate from 80C.
- **80CCD(2)**: Employer's NPS contribution up to 14% of salary — available under BOTH old and new regimes.

Combined: someone at 30% slab investing ₹50K in 80CCD(1B) saves ₹15,000 in tax. The NPS corpus grows largely tax-free and is locked till 60 — excellent for disciplined retirement saving.

## FD vs Debt MF — Tax Comparison

| Feature                  | Bank FD                     | Debt MF (pre-Apr 2023)     | Debt MF (post-Apr 2023)    |
|--------------------------|-----------------------------|----------------------------|----------------------------|
| Tax treatment            | Slab rate every year        | LTCG 12.5% after 36 months | Slab rate always (50AA)    |
| Indexation               | No                          | Yes (pre-Jul-2024 purchase)| No                         |
| TDS                      | 10% if interest > ₹40K/yr  | None                       | None                       |
| Lock-in                  | Penalty for early exit      | No                         | No                         |
| Best for?                | Short-term, predictable     | Medium-term (pre-Apr-23)   | No tax advantage over FD   |

**Bottom line for AY 2026-27**: Post-Apr-2023 debt MF has no tax advantage over FD. Use FDs for short-term needs, liquid funds for emergency corpus only.

## Capital Gains Account Scheme (CGAS) — Don't Miss the Deadline

If you've sold property and are eligible for Section 54/54EC/54F exemption but haven't yet made the investment before the ITR filing deadline:

1. Open a CGAS (Capital Gains Account Scheme) account at any nationalised bank.
2. Deposit the unutilised sale proceeds before the filing deadline (31 July 2026).
3. Withdraw only for the specified purpose (buying/constructing property, or 54EC bonds are separate).

Failure to deposit in CGAS before filing = the undeployed gain is TAXABLE that year.

## Avoiding Common Filing Mistakes

1. **Reconcile AIS and Form 26AS** before filing. Every income shown in AIS must appear in your return, or you'll get a notice.
2. **Dividends from stocks/MFs are fully taxable** at slab rate (since FY 2020-21). They appear in AIS. Don't miss them.
3. **Interest on FDs is taxable even if not withdrawn** — accrue annually and report under "Other Sources".
4. **If you switched jobs**, inform new employer of previous salary for correct TDS. Failure to do so leads to shortfall TDS and 234B interest.
5. **Aadhaar-PAN linking** is mandatory. Without it, your ITR may not be processed and you won't receive refunds.
6. **Pre-validate bank account** on the IT portal before filing for faster refund processing.
7. **E-verify within 30 days** of filing. Unverified returns are treated as invalid.

## Regime Switch — Key Rules

- **Salaried taxpayers**: Can switch between old and new regime EVERY year at the time of filing.
- **Business/profession income**: Can switch from old to new only ONCE (then locked in new regime).
- **Employer TDS**: Employer defaults to new regime if no declaration is made. You can still choose differently at filing — but you must pay any shortfall tax before filing.
- **Best practice**: Run engine comparison at start of FY (April) based on planned investments for the year, not at filing time.

## Salary Structuring — Work with HR

If your employer allows flexible benefits, restructuring your CTC can save tax under old regime:
- **Meal coupons/food allowance**: Up to ₹2,200/month (₹26,400/year) is tax-free.
- **LTA (Leave Travel Allowance)**: 2 trips in a 4-year block, economy class, within India — tax-free on actual expenses.
- **Telephone/internet reimbursement**: Actual bills reimbursed — tax-free.
- **Books and periodicals**: Actual reimbursement — tax-free.
- **Car allowance vs car perquisite**: Car used both officially and personally — flat perquisite value may be lower than actual perk value.

These are old-regime benefits only. Under new regime, all allowances are taxable.
